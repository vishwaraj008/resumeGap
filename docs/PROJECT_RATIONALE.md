# Why This Project

The obvious reason is because "everyone else is doing the movie recommender." But that would be too easy, too comfortable there's example data, it can be done in two days, and there's a step-by-step tutorial on how to do it. And that's exactly the reason why I didn't want to go down that road.

It was the recruiting angle that made this project interesting for me. If I'm already going to spend 8 days building an algorithm for matching system, it might as well try to solve a problem that's directly relevant to that process finding out whether a person is suitable for the role and, if not, what he or she needs. You can kind of solve it using free tools like open LinkedIn, checking the skills list attached to the job posting and seeing what you don't have. However, it's very basic, standardized for all candidates and just says "Here's what you need." It doesn't understand what specific competencies you bring to the table, how much each of those skills gaps matters compared to the other, and, above all, doesn't tell you what to learn first and how much time it will take.

This takes on the following form concretely: an individual who has had three years of experience in working with Excel and reporting skills, with an intent to land a job of a Data Analyst, uploads his or her CV and receives a 71% match, a list of things that are required with regard to SQL and Power BI skills, among others, at the top of this list and a six-week sequence plan to fill this gap using both free and paid sources of information. While developing this solution, I kept in my mind a concrete user, namely, an individual who has worked for a few years in a position in India and is now trying to understand whether a move to the desired position is possible and how can this be done.

To begin with, it has to be acknowledged right away that my NER model performs far from perfectly. It shows a score of 47% F1. I have tried to raise it by trying various base models and by extending the number of training iterations but without much success. I was facing a dilemma should I spend more time on improving the number to make it look good, or should I take it as it is and adjust other components of the system accordingly. Obviously, the latter option prevailed. The system will now use a second, independent keyword extraction algorithm based on the list of skills along with the NER model.

Matching is not just an easy checklist as well. At first, I almost released the initial draft of the algorithm with all the skills being weighted equally towards the matching, when it suddenly hit me that the most common skills in the very first set were simply communication and teamwork - terms that are used in literally any job description ever written and hence are not informative regarding the match. This was an interesting lesson learned. The final release weights all the skills according to their importance in each role in particular, which is the same TF-IDF signal the algorithm uses for matching, meaning that a very rare but vital skill of Kubernetes would be worth much more than some common soft skill.

An advice that cannot be used in practice is really no advice at all, hence, this turned into a full-fledged containerized application auth, database behind it. The roadmap functionality is a good illustration of this concept: "you're lacking SQL" is no advice at all; this is just a statement. Every single gap will come along with an ordered next step and paid and unpaid resources, since telling a person to take up a paid course is no advice for a person who needs an unpaid course.

With additional time, the first thing to change would be the NER model itself, which would require a bigger and more diverse training set in order to go beyond 47% F1 and not rely on a keyword solution indefinitely. Next would be job market data that could update itself automatically rather than relying on a fixed dataset for training.

The common thread running through all of this is that my good ideas usually came after something broke down. Problems with soft skills, with a dataset lacking a piece of information I thought would be there, with a low NER score nothing of it was expected and yet each one influenced the outcome more than anything else.
